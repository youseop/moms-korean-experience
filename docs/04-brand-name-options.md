# Brand Name Options — Mom's Korean Experience

> Brainstorm for the project's brand name. 5 vibe categories × 5 options = 25 names, plus a recommendation at the end. The brand should lean strongly on the "Mom" concept — not a hotel, not a tour outfit, but "stay with a Korean mom."

Locked context for evaluation:
- Mom's name is **Eunjung 은정**.
- Audience is international travelers (especially Korean-language students) who want a deep-local Jeongja experience.
- English-only at launch; Hangul allowed as a design accent.
- Tone: warm, maternal, "like visiting your Korean mom."

Domains listed below are guesses, not availability checks.

---

## Category 1 — Mom-forward, English-clean

The simplest play: lean on "Mom" in plain English. Easy to spell, easy to remember, easy to say to a friend on the subway. The risk is genericness — half the food bloggers in Korea already have "Mom's Kitchen" in their handle.

**Name:** "Korean Mom"
- **Tagline option:** "Tours, cooking, and a room — in Jeongja, Seongnam."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** korean-mom.com, koreanmom.co, withkoreanmom.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Direct, almost too direct — declarative and unambiguous.
- **Strengths:**
  - Says exactly what it is in two words; impossible to forget.
  - Strong SEO opportunity for a long-tail phrase tourists actually type.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - Generic enough that it could read like a content farm or a recipe blog, not a real person.
  - "koreanmom.com" is almost certainly taken; you'll be on a hyphen or a .co.

**Name:** "Mom in Jeongja"
- **Tagline option:** "A Korean mom, her kitchen, her favorite places, and a room."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** mominjeongja.com, mom-in-jeongja.com
- **Vibe in one line:** A book title. Locates Mom in a real place.
- **Strengths:**
  - Specific without being inside-baseball — even non-Koreans can roughly say "jeong-ja."
  - Implicitly anchors the stay/cooking/tours to one neighborhood, which is honest.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - "Jeongja" is the spelling barrier — guests will misspell it, can't share by voice easily.
  - "Mom in [place]" reads slightly like a documentary; less brandable than a possessive.

**Name:** "Mom's Day Off"
- **Tagline option:** "Spend a day in Korea like family. Tours, cooking, and a place to sleep."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** momsdayoff.com, momsdayoff.co
- **Vibe in one line:** Playful, gift-shop tone — a day designed by Mom, for you.
- **Strengths:**
  - Memorable phrase with built-in warmth; doubles as a marketing concept ("today is your mom's day off — let mine take over").
  - Easy to say and spell in English.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - "Day off" implies single-day; clashes with the Stay (multi-night) product.
  - Slightly cute; might undersell the depth of the cooking/stay experiences.

**Name:** "The Mom Trip"
- **Tagline option:** "Korea, slowly, with a mom who knows the good places."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** themomtrip.com, momtrip.co
- **Vibe in one line:** Friendly, indie, podcast-ready.
- **Strengths:**
  - "Trip" frames the whole offering (tour + stay + cooking) as one experience, not three SKUs.
  - Sounds like something a 20-something traveler would screenshot and send to a friend.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - "Mom trip" in American English can read as "trip with my own mom" — a brief beat of confusion.
  - .com almost certainly squatted; might end up on a weirder TLD.

**Name:** "Like Mom's"
- **Tagline option:** "A Korean home in Jeongja — meals, places, a room."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** likemoms.com, likemoms.co, likemomsjeongja.com
- **Vibe in one line:** A phrase, almost a sigh — "this tastes like Mom's."
- **Strengths:**
  - Universally evocative; everyone has a "like Mom's" food memory.
  - Two short syllables, instantly memorable.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - Possessive-without-noun is grammatically a little odd in English ("Like Mom's what?").
  - Doesn't communicate Korea or place at all — needs the tagline to do all the work.

## Category 2 — Eunjung's name as the brand

The most personal angle. You're not a brand, you're a person. Risk: "Eunjung" is a five-letter Korean name most English speakers will pause on the first time they read it. Reward: massive trust signal, and zero competition for the exact name.

**Name:** "Eunjung's Table"
- **Tagline option:** "Korean home cooking, hidden corners, and a quiet room in Jeongja."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** eunjungstable.com, eunjungs.com, eunjung.kitchen
- **Vibe in one line:** Personal, dinner-table-warm, hospitality-led.
- **Strengths:**
  - "Table" is universal hospitality language — implies food, conversation, welcome.
  - Personal name signals "this is a real person, not a company."
- **Weaknesses:**
  - "Eunjung" reads as four syllables to a non-Korean and slows the first impression.
  - "Table" foregrounds cooking — Tours (the priority-1 product) gets pushed to the background.

**Name:** "Eunjung's House"
- **Tagline option:** "Stay, cook, and explore Jeongja with Eunjung."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** eunjungshouse.com, eunjungs.house, houseofeunjung.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Homestay-direct, B&B-shaped, friendly.
- **Strengths:**
  - "House" cleanly contains all three products (you can stay, cook in it, leave from it for tours).
  - Reads almost like a Brooklyn café or a small inn — current brand vocabulary.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - "House" pushes the Stay product to the front, even though Tours is priority 1.
  - Slight overlap with "Eunjung Guesthouse" type brands that already exist in Korea.

**Name:** "Eunjung & You"
- **Tagline option:** "Days in Korea, hosted by Mom."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** eunjungandyou.com, eunjung-and-you.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Warm, two-person, almost like a duo brand.
- **Strengths:**
  - The "& You" reframes the brand as a relationship, not a service.
  - Distinct from any existing Korean tour or stay brand naming.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - "Eunjung and You" is six syllables; awkward in casual speech.
  - Feels mildly self-help-y; could read as a coaching brand.

**Name:** "Eunjung 은정"
- **Tagline option:** "Tours, cooking, and stay with a Korean mom in Jeongja."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** eunjung.kr, eunjung.co, eunjung.house
- **Vibe in one line:** Just the name. Confident, monogram-like.
- **Strengths:**
  - One-name brand (think "Cher", "Ina") — extremely memorable if you can pull it off.
  - The Hangul sitting next to the romanization becomes a logo and a teaching moment.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - Audience won't know what "Eunjung" *is* without context everywhere on the page.
  - Hard to talk about offline — "go check out Eunjung" without context falls flat.

**Name:** "Letters from Eunjung"
- **Tagline option:** "A Korean mom in Jeongja sharing her city, her kitchen, and her home."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** lettersfromeunjung.com, fromeunjung.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Substacky, intimate, slightly literary.
- **Strengths:**
  - "Letters from" is warm, inviting, and built-in content brand (newsletter, blog, IG captions).
  - Differentiates strongly from any existing tour/stay brand voice.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - Suggests a publication more than a service — guests might not know they can *book* something.
  - Long URL; awkward to print on a card.

## Category 3 — Korean-flavored (Hangul or Korean word)

Korean word in the name. Higher friction for English speakers, but signals authenticity and gives the brand a sound that "Mom's House" never will. Best paired with an English subtitle that translates immediately.

**Name:** "Umma's"
- **Tagline option:** "엄마's. A Korean mom in Jeongja — meals, tours, and a room."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** ummas.co, ummas.house, ummas.kr
- **Vibe in one line:** Diaspora-friendly, single-word, café-shaped.
- **Strengths:**
  - "Umma" is one of the few Korean words a Western audience already half-knows from K-content.
  - Possessive form ("Umma's") makes it a place — Umma's [house, table, kitchen] all implied.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - "Umma's" without a noun is even rougher in English than "Like Mom's" — needs strong typography to feel intentional.
  - Already used by various Korean restaurants worldwide; not unique.

**Name:** "Umma House"
- **Tagline option:** "엄마집. Mom's home in Jeongja — stay, cook, explore Korea."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** ummahouse.com, umma.house, ummahousejeongja.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Direct translation of 엄마집 — homestay with a heart.
- **Strengths:**
  - Bilingual brand — works in English AND looks great in Hangul on a sign.
  - "House" implies all three products (food, place, base for tours).
- **Weaknesses:**
  - "Umma" requires explanation for first-timers who don't know any Korean.
  - Sounds slightly like "OOMA" the VoIP company, which dominates the search results.

**Name:** "Jeongja Jip" (정자집)
- **Tagline option:** "Mom's home in Jeongja. Korean cooking, local tours, a quiet room."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** jeongjajip.com, jeongja.jp (no — wrong country code), jeongjahouse.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Hyper-local, Hangul-friendly, neighborhood-scale.
- **Strengths:**
  - "Jip" (집 = house) is short and rhymes-ish with "Jeongja"; phonetically catchy.
  - Maximum "I am a real place in a real neighborhood" signal.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - Two unfamiliar Korean words back-to-back — heavy spelling/pronunciation load.
  - "Jip" looks like a typo of "trip" or a name in English; will need explanation.

**Name:** "Eomma & Jeongja"
- **Tagline option:** "엄마 & 정자. A Korean mom and her neighborhood — yours for a few days."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** eommajeongja.com, eomma-jeongja.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Two characters, two anchors — person + place.
- **Strengths:**
  - "Eomma" (엄마) is the more accurate romanization than "Umma"; signals real Korean knowledge.
  - Conjunction format ("X & Y") reads like a brand, not a description.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - "Eomma" is harder to pronounce than "Umma" for English speakers despite being more accurate.
  - Two Korean words = double the friction.

**Name:** "집 / Jip"
- **Tagline option:** "Home, in Korean. A mom's place in Jeongja — meals, tours, a room."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** jip.house, jip.kr, jipjeongja.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Minimalist, design-forward, single-syllable.
- **Strengths:**
  - One syllable, impossible to forget once explained — "jip means home."
  - The Hangul character 집 is visually striking and works as a logo on its own.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - In English, "Jip" reads as gibberish or a typo until decoded.
  - Doesn't communicate "Mom" at all — undermines the locked brand direction.

## Category 4 — Editorial / poetic

Evocative phrase, not a description. Reads like a guidebook chapter or a New Yorker essay title. Trades clarity for atmosphere — risky if your booking funnel needs the name to do explanatory work, but powerful if the photography and copy carry the brand.

**Name:** "A Quiet Door in Jeongja"
- **Tagline option:** "A Korean mom's home, opened to travelers. Meals, tours, a room."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** quietdoor.co, quietdoorjeongja.com, aquietdoor.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Slow-travel essay, half-whispered.
- **Strengths:**
  - Evokes the actual physical thing — you knock, a door opens, you're in.
  - Distinctive in a search result page full of "Seoul Tour Co." names.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - Doesn't say "mom," "Korea," or "stay" — relies entirely on tagline + photography.
  - "Quiet door" sounds slightly like a meditation retreat or a wellness brand.

**Name:** "The Long Table"
- **Tagline option:** "Dinner with Mom in Jeongja, every night you stay. Tours and cooking too."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** thelongtable.co, longtablejeongja.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Communal, food-centered, hospitality-magazine.
- **Strengths:**
  - "Long table" is shared-meal language — instantly evokes the daily-dinner feature of the Stay.
  - Memorable, easy to say, easy to spell.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - Doesn't communicate Korea — could be a farm-to-table place anywhere.
  - "The Long Table" is a fairly common restaurant name worldwide; SEO will be hard.

**Name:** "Soft Seoul"
- **Tagline option:** "The Korea your mom would show you. Tours, cooking, and a stay in Jeongja."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** softseoul.com, soft-seoul.com, softseoul.co
- **Vibe in one line:** Slow Seoul, gentle counter-programming to neon nightlife Korea.
- **Strengths:**
  - "Soft" is doing real positioning work — opposite of Hongdae, opposite of Itaewon.
  - Two-word, alliterative, very brandable for IG and merch.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - The brand isn't actually in Seoul — it's in Seongnam (technically Gyeonggi). Slight honesty problem.
  - "Soft" is overused in startup branding; not as fresh as it would have been five years ago.

**Name:** "Across from Tancheon"
- **Tagline option:** "A Korean mom's apartment by the stream — meals, tours, a room."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** acrossfromtancheon.com, fromtancheon.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Specific, almost overheard — like a friend giving directions.
- **Strengths:**
  - Tied to a real place feature (the Tancheon stream) that makes the location feel earned.
  - "Across from [landmark]" is the kind of phrase travelers actually remember.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - "Tancheon" is a barrier word — most English speakers won't know what it is.
  - Long; doesn't fit on most logo lockups.

**Name:** "The Spare Room"
- **Tagline option:** "A Korean mom in Jeongja kept a room for you. Stay, cook, explore."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** thespareroom.co, thespareroomjeongja.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Domestic, modest, almost embarrassed in a charming way.
- **Strengths:**
  - Captures the actual offer — the Stay is literally a spare room in a family apartment.
  - Self-effacing tone is the opposite of polished hotel branding; very on-brand.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - "Spare Room" is a UK rental marketplace (SpareRoom.com) — will lose every search.
  - Frames the brand around the Stay (priority 3), not Tours (priority 1).

## Category 5 — Place-led

Anchored to the neighborhood. Most useful if you want the brand to grow with the neighborhood ("come to Jeongja") rather than the person. Risk: travelers don't know Jeongja yet, so the brand has to teach the place at the same time it sells the experience.

**Name:** "Jeongja Days"
- **Tagline option:** "A few days in a real Korean neighborhood — with Mom."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** jeongjadays.com, jeongja-days.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Travel-diary, episode-of-your-life framing.
- **Strengths:**
  - "Days" frames the experience as time, not transactions — fits tour/cooking/stay equally well.
  - Easy to say once "Jeongja" is learned; pleasant rhythm.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - "Jeongja" is the front door; travelers must learn to spell it before they can find you.
  - Mom is invisible in the name itself — needs heavy lifting from imagery.

**Name:** "Mom of Jeongja"
- **Tagline option:** "Tours, cooking, and stays — hosted by a real Korean mom in Seongnam."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** momofjeongja.com, themomofjeongja.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Title-of-a-person, like "Mayor of Jeongja" — affectionate.
- **Strengths:**
  - Combines the locked "Mom" requirement with the place anchor — best of both worlds.
  - "Mom of [place]" is unusual phrasing in English; sticks in the head.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - "Mom of Jeongja" sounds like a documentary or a community-newspaper feature.
  - Slightly overclaims — there are many moms in Jeongja.

**Name:** "Tancheon Mom"
- **Tagline option:** "Meals, tours, and a room by Seoul's quietest stream."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** tancheonmom.com, tancheon-mom.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Two-word, river-anchored, distinctive.
- **Strengths:**
  - "Tancheon" is geographically specific in a way "Seoul" isn't — earns the local-expertise claim.
  - Alliterative-ish and short; fits well as an Instagram handle.
- **Weaknesses:**
  - Travelers don't know Tancheon by name; you'll explain it on every page.
  - "Tancheon" is the hardest of the three Korean place words to say (T-an-chuhn).

**Name:** "South of Seoul"
- **Tagline option:** "A Korean mom's home in Jeongja — meals, tours, and a room, just south of Seoul."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** southofseoul.com, southofseoul.co
- **Vibe in one line:** Geographic positioning — outside the obvious.
- **Strengths:**
  - Solves the "Jeongja is hard to spell" problem by triangulating from a place travelers know.
  - Implicitly says "this is the deeper-than-Seoul experience you came looking for."
- **Weaknesses:**
  - "South of Seoul" is already used by a Korea-focused travel blog/podcast (a real brand).
  - Doesn't communicate Mom or food at all — name is purely geographic.

**Name:** "Seongnam Sundays"
- **Tagline option:** "Slow days in a Korean neighborhood — Mom's cooking, Mom's tours, Mom's room."
- **Domain options (check availability later):** seongnamsundays.com, seongnam-sundays.com
- **Vibe in one line:** Lazy-weekend energy, alliterative, gentle.
- **Strengths:**
  - "Sundays" suggests rest, food, family — exactly the brand mood.
  - "Seongnam" is the official city name; technically more accurate than "Seoul."
- **Weaknesses:**
  - Implies Sunday-only, which is wrong — guests stay any day of the week.
  - "Seongnam" is even less recognized internationally than "Jeongja."

## Recommendation

### Top 3 picks

**1. "Eunjung's Table" (Category 2)**
Carries the strongest emotional payload in the fewest words. "Table" is doing brand work in three directions at once — cooking class (literally a table), stay (the daily home-cooked dinner), tours (you come back to the table at the end of the day). Personal-name brand reads as honest in a category overrun by template-built tour companies. The cost is that "Eunjung" forces a small mental beat from a non-Korean reader on first contact, and "Table" centers the food at the expense of Tours, the priority-1 product. Manageable: lead with a Tours photo in the hero and let the table metaphor do warmth, not navigation.

**2. "Mom of Jeongja" (Category 5)**
The cleanest combination of the locked "Mom" preference and a real place anchor. It sounds like a title — "Mayor of Jeongja," "Mom of Jeongja" — which gives Eunjung a kind of folkloric authority that "Korean Mom" doesn't. Travelers immediately understand the proposition (mom + neighborhood = local insider). The trade is that "Jeongja" is a spelling friction every reader has to push through once, and that "of" framing has a faint documentary tone that some might read as performative rather than personal. Worth it for the specificity.

**3. "Umma House" (Category 3)**
The most "actually Korean" option that's still legible to a Western audience. "Umma" is one of the few Korean words K-content has already taught the internet, and "House" is universal. Bilingual lockup (엄마집) is a designer's gift — works on a sign, a card, a t-shirt. The trade is that "Umma" is a soft-mode bet — assumes the reader is at least K-curious, not a complete Korea novice. For the actual target audience (Korean-language students and repeat Korea travelers), that bet is safe and probably a strength.

### If forced to ship today

**"Eunjung's Table."**

It's the option I'd bet on for emotional conversion. The audience for this site is not someone googling "Korea tours" cold — it's someone who has already been to Korea, has a recommendation from a friend, and is reading the homepage to decide if Mom is *real*. A first name on the door does that work faster than any other framing. Tours-vs-table is a real concern, but it's solvable in design (hero a tours photo, name in a quiet serif). The intimacy is unforgeable.

### What you're trading with each

- **Eunjung's Table** — trades broad-search discoverability and Tours-first framing for personal warmth and a single unforgettable hospitality metaphor.
- **Mom of Jeongja** — trades effortless pronunciation (Jeongja friction) for hyper-specific local positioning and a memorable title-of-a-person framing.
- **Umma House** — trades complete-novice accessibility for cultural authenticity and a bilingual lockup that designs itself.

### What I'd rule out fast

- "Korean Mom" and "Like Mom's" — too generic, too crowded, will lose every search.
- "Jip" / "Jeongja Jip" — too much spelling load on a Western reader; cool in person, fragile online.
- "South of Seoul" — already a real brand; you'll spend years out-ranking them.
- "The Spare Room" — SpareRoom.com owns this term in search.

### Next step

Pick the top 1–2 you respond to emotionally, then check domain availability and Instagram handle availability before falling in love. The doc deliberately does not pre-validate either — half of these are probably squatted, and the answer to "which name" depends partly on which `.com` is actually buyable for under $100.
